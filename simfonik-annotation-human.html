Chapter 013 — full body listening and fake listening
013_01_STU Theory framing — both players Shared audio ✓ annotated
Script
"A key part of empathy is listening. Sometimes we think we are listening but actually we are fooling ourselves and others all the time. It's easy to fake listen. To be listened to is a striking experience partly because it is so rare. The real problem with fake listening is that patients know it; they can feel it. It isn't authentic, it isn't engaged, and it doesn't build trust with your patient. We want to listen with our intuition. We want to hear what the patient is NOT saying. Because patients open up when they know they're really being listened to… Fake listening is where our awareness is primarily on ourselves and our self-talk: our own thoughts, feelings, judgments and conclusions. We can appear to be listening and even make encouraging noises — but our internal dialogue is quite different. When we seek to engage and empathise with a patient we need to move beyond our own thoughts and needs. Let's try some fake listening."

Chapter 014 — real listening
014_01_STU Theory — full body listening defined Shared audio ✓ annotated
Script
"Real listening, also known as full body listening, is listening without our own thoughts or personal agenda intruding. We notice what the other person says and how they say it — also picking up on what is unsaid. It includes non-verbal cues: leaning forward and making eye contact, for example. In full body listening all of our awareness is intently on the other person: their words, tone of voice, pauses, facial expressions, posture, emotions, energy and rhythm. We notice what lights them up and what deflates them. Our focus is so fixed on them that our own mental chatter vanishes and we speak with spontaneity. We also notice how our responses impact the other person."

Chapter 016 — practice good listening
016_01_STU_A / 016_01_STU_B Full-body listening — A attends, B recalls Split audio ✓ annotated
Player A hears
"I want you to imagine your whole body is an ear and you are going to listen with your whole body. Remember you cannot be doing anything else if you are truly listening to someone. Notice the change in the way you are listening and be sure to focus on everything they say. Afterwards you will need to repeat it back to them. Ask your partner to tell you about a time where they were not listened to. Go 5, 4, 3, 2, 1"
Player B hears
"Think about a time in your life when you felt you were not being heard, where you were not being listened to. When asked, tell your partner about this time in your life. Try to talk about as many details that you can remember. Try to recall who was involved and what the power dynamics were in the situation and the consequences for you. But most importantly try to remember how it made you feel, and explain this to your partner. Keep talking until you hear the signal to stop. Bring your sentence to an end. 5, 4, 3, 2, 1"
