The inject catalogue — a plain-English guide

SIMFONIK / SIM MAKER · v4 · May 2026

Every line of audio in a SIMFONIK scenario is an inject — a single instruction delivered to a player at a specific moment. The inject catalogue is the reference that names, distinguishes, and explains every type of inject used across the scripts. Think of it as the grammar book for the SIMFONIK language: it doesn't write the sentences, but it tells you what every kind of word does and when to use it.

What is an inject?

An inject is any audio instruction that lands in a player's earpiece during a session. It might tell them to do something physical, say something out loud, think something internally, observe their partner, or simply wait. Every chapter of a SIMFONIK script is made up of a sequence of injects, layered and timed so the two players experience a coordinated interaction — even though they're often hearing completely different things.

A good inject rule of thumb: at every moment, each player should know exactly what they're supposed to think, notice, say, do, withhold, or wait for. If they're guessing, the inject hasn't done its job — unless the not-knowing is deliberate.

The five questions every inject answers

Each inject is described along five dimensions. Together, these five questions give you a complete picture of what a given line is doing and why.

What is it doing?
Its function — the job it performs. There are 32 named function types, grouped into 7 families (see below).
How does it sound?
Its tone — neutral, empathetic, authoritative, provocative, flat, charged, and so on.
Where does it come from?
Its source — the narrator, a character, the player's own internal voice, the platform system, the supervisor, ambient sound.
When does it arrive?
Its timing — linear (in sequence), layered (running underneath something else), timed, simultaneous with the other player's stream, staggered, interruptive.
What does it do to the player?
Its relational impact — empowers, frames, regulates, pressures, discomforts, shifts perspective, amplifies voice, creates dissonance, and so on.

Most injects only exercise three or four of these dimensions explicitly — the others are implied by context. But all five are there if you look for them.

The six mode tags — what the player is being asked to do

Every inject gets one primary mode tag. This is the most practical label in the whole system — the one that lives at the script level and tells you, at a glance, what kind of response the inject is calling for.

ACT
Take a visible physical action.
"Stand up and move your chair next to them."
SPEAK
Say something out loud — exact words or own words.
"Say: I can't sleep. I wake up all the time."
REFLECT
Think — internal framing, emotional context, memory.
"You know what it's like. You've been through some stuff."
ATTEND
Observe — focus on the partner's behaviour, tone, body.
"How are they behaving? Can you get a sense of their emotions?"
PRESSURE
Adds urgency, tension, or constraint.
"You have one minute left."
PAUSE
Designed silence — the absence of audio is intentional.
"Dead space." / no audio for 8 seconds.

An inject can carry a secondary mode tag if two things are happening at once — but if two functions compete equally, the guidance is to split them into two consecutive injects rather than cram both into one.

The seven function families — what injects are for

The 32 individual inject types are grouped into seven families. The families are organised roughly in order of when they tend to appear in a session — you always open with A, you always regulate with B before demanding emotional work, and the roleplay and voice work in G tend to come later.

Family What it covers In plain English
A — Session management Welcome, identity framing, safety, participation rules, open and close. The scaffolding around the experience — everything that isn't scenario content but holds the scenario together.
B — Safety & regulation Safety scans, somatic grounding, breath work, body wake-up, co-regulation, consent, opt-down routes. Settling the nervous system before and after demanding content. The scripts run a regulation beat between every major piece of emotional work — this is not optional ornament.
C — Relational setup Seating, pair acknowledgement, role allocation, proximity. Establishing who the two players are to each other, where they are in space, and what is unequal between them. The structural conditions that make the scenario possible.
D — Reflective & cognitive Self-reflection, memory recall, pain recall, anti-assumption, ethical injects, internal distraction. Work that happens inside the player's head — framing, memory, doubt, and the occasional deliberate disruption of thought (the fake-listening monologue is the prime example).
E — Attention & observation Partner observation, emotional inference, soft focus, eye-contact training, listening posture, full-body listening, fake listening, repeat-back. Directing what the player looks at and how. The foundational noticing skills that empathy depends on — usually developed through contrast: the wrong version first, then the right version.
F — Embodied & relational Face training, kind eyes, three types of mirroring (breath, movement, posture), mimicry warning, therapeutic touch sequence. Empathy delivered through the body — face, breath, posture, gesture, and physical contact. The layer where everything else becomes visible.
G — Voice, dialogue & roleplay Vocal prosody, meet-and-match, de-escalation arc, exact lines, own-words speech, scenario handover, improvisation release, roleplay closure, peer feedback, swap-role, debrief, designed silence, cues. The largest family by far, because voice and roleplay carry most of the scenario weight. Everything from "say this exact phrase" to "the roleplay is yours now."

How injects work together

Layering

Some injects don't replace each other — they run simultaneously. The most striking example in the scripts is the fake-listening exercise: Player A receives an instruction to pretend to listen (E7), while a continuous stream of mundane inner-monologue questions (D6) plays underneath in the same earpiece at the same time. Neither inject is optional; the combination is the pedagogy.

Simultaneous-paired streaming

At any given moment, Player A and Player B may be hearing completely different injects. One player might be receiving a pain recall (D3) while the other is being told to listen with their whole body (E6). The platform routes different audio to different earpieces at the same beat — this asymmetry is core to how SIMFONIK works.

The contrast principle

Many of the most important injects only work in pairs — specifically, by experiencing the wrong version before the right one. Kind eyes works because you've felt the threat of squinting. Full-body listening lands because you've just experienced what fake listening feels like from the inside. The catalogue calls these diptychs, and recognising them is essential to understanding why the script is sequenced the way it is.

The key pattern to remember: SIMFONIK rarely teaches a concept by stating it. It teaches by getting you to feel it — usually through contrast. The inject that comes before the lesson is often just as important as the lesson itself.

The de-escalation arc

One of the most carefully designed inject sequences in the catalogue is the de-escalation arc — used whenever a scenario involves an agitated or distressed person. The counterintuitive move it teaches is: meet the person at their intensity first before attempting to calm them. Going straight to calm reads as patronising and makes things worse. The arc is scripted in labelled percentage steps — 100%, 25%, 10%, 0% — with each step only permitted once the other person has softened.

"Oh no! I am not surprised that you're upset!!!" — the nurse meets the relative at 100%, rather than "Oh dear. Let's calm down."

Common mistakes the catalogue guards against

The catalogue includes a named list of anti-patterns — things that commonly go wrong in script-writing. A few worth knowing:

The premature-calm anti-pattern. Going straight to a quiet voice with a 100%-charged person. The other person reads it as dismissive and escalates further.
The overloaded inject. Trying to do reflection, framing, and instruction in one line. If it's doing three things, split it into three injects.
The consent tick-box. Asking for consent once at the start and forgetting it. Consent is live — it re-enters whenever the conditions shift.
The unscaffolded improvisation. Handing the roleplay over to the player before they have enough scripted handles to know where they are in the scene. Improvisation release only works after the player has been given enough structure to improvise from.
The skipped withdrawal. Ending a therapeutic touch sequence without an explicit phase for removing the hand. The withdrawal is a separate inject, not an implied close.

The one-line version

The inject catalogue is a named vocabulary for every move in a SIMFONIK script — what each line does, who delivers it, when it arrives, and what it produces in the person hearing it. Its job is to make the tacit logic of a Jaji-written scenario legible enough that someone else — or an AI — can understand, replicate, and eventually extend it.
